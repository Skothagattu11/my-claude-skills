# Yogic Ethics and Practice Structure

## Knowledge Base Document 2/6

A comprehensive reference for yoga's ethical foundation and practice frameworks.

---

## 1. The Yamas: Social Ethics

### Overview

The yamas are the first limb of Patañjali's aṣṭāṅga yoga. They constitute the ethical foundation—the "great vow" (mahāvrata) that applies universally, regardless of time, place, or circumstance.

**Key Principle:** Ethics come BEFORE practice. The yamas are prerequisites, not optional add-ons.

### The Five Yamas

#### 1. Ahiṃsā (अहिंसा) - Non-violence

**Definition:** Abstaining from causing harm to any living being through thought, word, or deed.

**Scope:**
- Physical harm
- Verbal harm (harsh words, gossip)
- Mental harm (ill wishes, judgment)
- Harm to self
- Harm through negligence or indifference

**Practical Applications:**

| Domain | Ahiṃsā Practice |
|--------|-----------------|
| Diet | Consider impact of food choices |
| Speech | Avoid harsh, critical, or harmful words |
| Thought | Notice and redirect violent thoughts |
| Action | Prevent unnecessary harm |
| Environment | Minimize ecological damage |
| Self | Avoid self-harm, self-criticism |

**Subtlety:** Ahiṃsā doesn't mean passivity. Protecting the vulnerable may require force. The intent matters.

**Modern Application:**
- Non-violent communication (NVC)
- Ethical consumption
- Compassionate self-talk
- Conflict resolution skills

---

#### 2. Satya (सत्य) - Truthfulness

**Definition:** Alignment of thought, word, and deed with truth and reality.

**Scope:**
- Not lying
- Not exaggerating
- Not omitting relevant truth
- Not deceiving through implication
- Being true to oneself

**Practical Applications:**

| Domain | Satya Practice |
|--------|---------------|
| Speech | Speak only what is true |
| Thought | Cultivate accurate perception |
| Self-presentation | Authentic representation |
| Commitments | Only promise what you'll deliver |
| Self-knowledge | Honest self-assessment |

**The Satya-Ahiṃsā Balance:**

Truth should not be used as a weapon. The guidance:
> "Speak the truth. Speak what is pleasant. Do not speak truth that is unpleasant. Do not speak pleasant falsehood."

If truth would cause harm without benefit, silence may be appropriate.

**Modern Application:**
- Radical honesty (tempered with compassion)
- Integrity in business
- Authentic relationships
- Accurate self-perception

---

#### 3. Asteya (अस्तेय) - Non-stealing

**Definition:** Not taking what is not freely given.

**Scope:**
- Material possessions
- Time (being late, wasting others' time)
- Attention (dominating conversations)
- Ideas (intellectual property)
- Credit (for others' work)
- Energy (emotional vampirism)
- Opportunities (taking what's meant for others)

**Practical Applications:**

| Domain | Asteya Practice |
|--------|----------------|
| Material | Don't take what isn't offered |
| Time | Be punctual, respect others' schedules |
| Attention | Listen as much as speak |
| Ideas | Give credit, cite sources |
| Work | Fair compensation, no exploitation |
| Relationships | Don't take more than you give |

**Subtle Form:** Wanting what others have (desire is pre-theft).

**Modern Application:**
- Fair business practices
- Time management respect
- Intellectual honesty
- Not over-consuming

---

#### 4. Brahmacarya (ब्रह्मचर्य) - Wise Use of Energy

**Definition:** Literally "walking in Brahman" or "conduct becoming of a seeker of Brahman." Traditionally interpreted as celibacy; more broadly, wise management of vital energy.

**Scope:**
- Sexual energy (conservation or conscious use)
- Creative energy
- Emotional energy
- Attention/focus
- Life force generally

**Practical Applications:**

| Domain | Brahmacarya Practice |
|--------|---------------------|
| Sexual | Mindful, sacred approach to sexuality |
| Media | Limit overstimulation |
| Social | Quality over quantity of interactions |
| Work | Focused effort, avoid scattered energy |
| Food | Eat for energy, not overeating |
| Rest | Adequate sleep and recovery |

**Modern Interpretation:** Not necessarily celibacy, but conscious relationship with desire and energy expenditure. Ask: "Is this use of my energy moving me toward my highest goals?"

**Modern Application:**
- Digital minimalism
- Energy management
- Conscious sexuality
- Focus practices

---

#### 5. Aparigraha (अपरिग्रह) - Non-grasping

**Definition:** Not accumulating beyond what is necessary; freedom from greed and possessiveness.

**Scope:**
- Material possessions
- Relationships (possessiveness)
- Ideas (attachment to being right)
- Outcomes (attachment to results)
- Identity (attachment to self-image)
- The past (holding on to grievances)

**Practical Applications:**

| Domain | Aparigraha Practice |
|--------|---------------------|
| Material | Own only what you need and use |
| Emotional | Release grievances, don't cling |
| Relational | Love without possessing |
| Work | Effort without attachment to outcome |
| Beliefs | Hold views loosely |
| Past | Let go, don't accumulate resentment |

**The Practice:** Regular review of what you're holding onto—material, emotional, mental—and conscious release.

**Modern Application:**
- Minimalism
- Emotional decluttering
- Outcome independence
- Abundance mindset (release scarcity grasping)

## 2. The Niyamas: Personal Observances

### Overview

The niyamas are the second limb—personal practices that cultivate inner growth. While yamas focus outward (social ethics), niyamas focus inward (personal discipline).

### The Five Niyamas

#### 1. Śauca (शौच) - Purity/Cleanliness

**Definition:** Cleanliness and purity of body, mind, and environment.

**Scope:**
- Physical body (hygiene, diet)
- Mind (mental clarity, pure thoughts)
- Speech (clean language)
- Environment (orderly space)
- Relationships (healthy associations)

**Practical Applications:**

| Domain | Śauca Practice |
|--------|---------------|
| Body | Daily hygiene rituals, clean diet |
| Mind | Limit mental junk food (news, negativity) |
| Speech | Avoid crude or polluting language |
| Space | Clean, organized environment |
| Input | Quality information and media |
| Social | Healthy, uplifting relationships |

**Modern Application:**
- Morning hygiene rituals
- Clean eating
- Information diet
- Space organization
- Marie Kondo principles

---

#### 2. Santoṣa (संतोष) - Contentment

**Definition:** Satisfaction with what is; not craving what is absent.

**Scope:**
- Acceptance of current circumstances
- Gratitude for what exists
- Freedom from constant wanting
- Peace with imperfection
- Finding sufficiency in the present

**The Paradox:** Contentment doesn't mean no goals. It means working toward goals without being miserable in the meantime.

**Practical Applications:**

| Domain | Santoṣa Practice |
|--------|-----------------|
| Material | Gratitude practice for possessions |
| Circumstance | Accept what cannot be changed |
| Progress | Appreciate the journey, not just destination |
| Comparison | Focus on own path, not others' |
| Present | Find completeness in this moment |

**Modern Application:**
- Daily gratitude practice
- Contentment meditation
- "Enough" mindset
- Comparison fasting

---

#### 3. Tapas (तपस्) - Discipline/Austerity

**Definition:** Literally "heat"—the fire of discipline that burns away impurities. Voluntary challenge and discomfort for growth.

**Scope:**
- Physical austerity (fasting, cold exposure, challenge)
- Mental discipline (focus, study, restraint)
- Speech discipline (silence, fasting from speech)
- Lifestyle simplicity
- Doing what is difficult but good

**Key Principle:** Tapas is voluntary, purposeful discomfort—not self-punishment.

**Practical Applications:**

| Domain | Tapas Practice |
|--------|---------------|
| Physical | Cold showers, fasting, challenging exercise |
| Mental | Deep focus sessions, difficult study |
| Speech | Periods of silence, restraint |
| Comfort | Simplify, reduce dependencies |
| Avoidance | Face what you've been avoiding |

**The Purpose:** Build will, burn karma (habitual patterns), create transformation capacity.

**Modern Application:**
- Intermittent fasting
- Cold exposure
- Digital fasts
- Voluntary hardship
- "Eat the frog" productivity

---

#### 4. Svādhyāya (स्वाध्याय) - Self-Study

**Definition:** Study of self and sacred texts. Two meanings:
1. Study of scriptures/wisdom traditions
2. Study of oneself (self-reflection)

**Scope:**
- Reading sacred/wisdom texts
- Recitation and contemplation
- Self-observation and reflection
- Journaling and introspection
- Understanding one's patterns

**Practical Applications:**

| Domain | Svādhyāya Practice |
|--------|-------------------|
| Reading | Daily wisdom text study |
| Recitation | Mantra, prayer, or passage memorization |
| Reflection | Journaling, contemplation |
| Pattern recognition | Noticing habits and tendencies |
| Feedback | Seeking input from others |
| Review | Regular life review practices |

**Modern Application:**
- Morning reading practice
- Journaling
- Therapy/coaching
- Meditation as self-observation
- 360-degree feedback

---

#### 5. Īśvara-praṇidhāna (ईश्वरप्रणिधान) - Surrender to the Divine

**Definition:** Dedication of actions and their fruits to something greater than ego. Surrender to divine will, higher purpose, or the sacred.

**Scope:**
- Offering actions as service
- Releasing attachment to outcomes
- Trust in something greater
- Humility before the sacred
- Acting as instrument rather than agent

**For Non-Theists:** Can be understood as:
- Surrender to the greater good
- Dedication to service
- Release of ego control
- Alignment with natural order
- Acting from values rather than personal gain

**Practical Applications:**

| Domain | Īśvara-praṇidhāna Practice |
|--------|---------------------------|
| Action | Offer work as service |
| Outcome | Release attachment to results |
| Control | Accept what cannot be controlled |
| Purpose | Align with something larger |
| Ego | Practice humility |
| Daily | Dedication/prayer practice |

**Modern Application:**
- Purpose-driven work
- Outcome independence
- Service orientation
- Humility practices
- Morning dedication

## 3. Aṣṭāṅga Yoga: The Eight Limbs

### Overview

The aṣṭāṅga (eight-limbed) yoga of Patañjali provides a complete path from ethical conduct to liberation. Crucially, the limbs are sequential—ethics come before poses.

### The Eight Limbs

```
EXTERNAL (Bahiraṅga)
├── 1. Yama - Social ethics
├── 2. Niyama - Personal observances
└── 3. Āsana - Posture/seat

INTERNAL (Antaraṅga)
├── 4. Prāṇāyāma - Breath control
├── 5. Pratyāhāra - Sense withdrawal
├── 6. Dhāraṇā - Concentration
├── 7. Dhyāna - Meditation
└── 8. Samādhi - Absorption

The last three together = SAṀYAMA
```

### Limb Details

| Limb | Sanskrit | Function | For Lifestyle Design |
|------|----------|----------|---------------------|
| 1. Yama | यम | Ethical restraints | Non-negotiable boundaries |
| 2. Niyama | नियम | Personal observances | Daily discipline structures |
| 3. Āsana | आसन | Steady, comfortable seat | Physical practice, embodiment |
| 4. Prāṇāyāma | प्राणायाम | Breath regulation | Energy management |
| 5. Pratyāhāra | प्रत्याहार | Sense withdrawal | Digital detox, input control |
| 6. Dhāraṇā | धारणा | Single-pointed focus | Deep work, concentration |
| 7. Dhyāna | ध्यान | Sustained meditation | Regular meditation practice |
| 8. Samādhi | समाधि | Absorption/union | Peak experiences, flow |

### Key Insight for Modern Application

**Modern yoga inverts the order:** Poses first, ethics maybe later (or never).

**Traditional order:** Ethics → Posture → Breath → Meditation → Liberation

**For lifestyle design:** Build ethical foundation first. Physical practice supports ethics, not replaces them.

## 4. Sādhanā: Life as Practice

### Definition

Sādhanā (from "sādh" = to accomplish) is spiritual practice—but more than that, it's the orientation toward life as continuous practice toward a goal.

### Characteristics of Sādhanā

| Characteristic | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Daily** | Done every day without exception |
| **Consistent** | Same time, same sequence |
| **Non-negotiable** | Not dependent on mood or circumstance |
| **Cumulative** | Benefits compound over time |
| **Sacred** | Elevated from mundane to meaningful |
| **Progressive** | Deepens with practice |

### Elements of a Complete Sādhanā

A traditional sādhanā might include:

| Element | Sanskrit | Practice Type |
|---------|----------|---------------|
| Purification | Śuddhi | Physical cleansing, space preparation |
| Invocation | Saṅkalpa | Setting intention |
| Body | Āsana | Physical practice |
| Breath | Prāṇāyāma | Breathing practices |
| Mantra | Japa | Recitation |
| Meditation | Dhyāna | Contemplation |
| Study | Svādhyāya | Reading/reflection |
| Dedication | Samarpana | Offering the practice |

### Designing Personal Sādhanā

**Minimum viable sādhanā** (15-20 minutes):
1. Purification (2 min) - Wash face, sit in clean space
2. Invocation (1 min) - Set intention, light candle
3. Body (5 min) - Simple movements or āsana
4. Breath (3 min) - Basic prāṇāyāma
5. Meditation (5 min) - Seated practice
6. Dedication (1 min) - Offer the benefits

**Expanded sādhanā** (60+ minutes):
- More elaborate versions of each element
- Add study (svādhyāya)
- Add mantra/japa
- Longer meditation

### Sādhanā vs. Routine

| Routine | Sādhanā |
|---------|---------|
| Habit | Practice |
| Optimizes | Transforms |
| Mechanical | Intentional |
| For efficiency | For growth |
| Can skip | Non-negotiable |
| Benefits me | Benefits all |

## 5. Integration with Behavioral Science

### Yamas as Habit Constraints

| Yama | Behavioral Function |
|------|---------------------|
| Ahiṃsā | Constraint on harm-causing behaviors |
| Satya | Constraint on deception behaviors |
| Asteya | Constraint on taking behaviors |
| Brahmacarya | Constraint on energy-depleting behaviors |
| Aparigraha | Constraint on accumulating behaviors |

**Key insight:** Yamas are not habits to build but boundaries that shape which habits are acceptable.

### Niyamas as Habit Categories

| Niyama | Habit Category |
|--------|---------------|
| Śauca | Cleanliness/organization habits |
| Santoṣa | Gratitude/contentment habits |
| Tapas | Challenge/discipline habits |
| Svādhyāya | Learning/reflection habits |
| Īśvara-praṇidhāna | Dedication/service habits |

**Design principle:** Ensure your habit portfolio includes all five niyama categories.

### Sādhanā as Keystone Habit

A morning sādhanā functions as what Charles Duhigg calls a "keystone habit"—a practice that creates cascading positive effects:

- Sets tone for the day
- Proves self-discipline capacity
- Creates momentum
- Connects to larger purpose
- Provides stable anchor amid chaos

Rate yourself 1-10 on each:

- [ ] Satya - Am I truthful and authentic?

## Key Takeaways for Lifestyle Architects

1. **Ethics are foundation, not decoration**: Yamas and niyamas come before āsana, before meditation, before everything else.

2. **Constraints before habits**: Define what's off-limits (yamas) before optimizing what you do.

3. **Balance all niyamas**: A complete life practice includes purity, contentment, discipline, study, and surrender.

4. **Sādhanā is non-negotiable**: The daily practice happens regardless of circumstances—that's what makes it transformative.

5. **Progress is sequential**: Don't rush to advanced practices while ethical foundations are shaky.

6. **Life as practice**: The goal isn't to have a practice but to make life itself the practice.
