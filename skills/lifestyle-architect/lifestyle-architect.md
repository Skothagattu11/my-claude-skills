# Lifestyle Architect Skill

## Designing Time-Tested, Science-Backed Lives

A comprehensive system that integrates ancient wisdom frameworks (refined over 3,000+ years) with modern behavioral science to design sustainable, meaningful lifestyle protocols.

---

## Language & Positioning

### Core Principle: Universal First, Origins Available

This skill uses **universal English terminology** as the primary language, with traditional Sanskrit terms available for enrichment. This ensures accessibility for global audiences while honoring the origins.

**See:** `knowledge/00-terminology-mapping.md` for complete mapping.

| Say This (Universal) | Traditional Term | Context |

### How to Present Origins

**DO:**
- Lead with universal terms
- Introduce traditional terms with: "This is based on an ancient practice called..."
- Connect to modern science: "Research now validates what these traditions intuited..."
- Position as "time-tested" and "refined over millennia"

**DON'T:**
- Lead with Sanskrit terminology
- Require users to learn or pronounce terms
- Use multiple Sanskrit terms in one sentence
- Position as "Indian" or "Hindu" — these are universal human insights

### Credibility Framing

```
GOOD:
- "Time-tested principles refined over 3,000+ years"
- "Ancient wisdom validated by modern science"
- "Practical philosophy from the world's oldest living tradition"

AVOID:
- "Hindu practices" (sounds religious/exclusive)
- "Indian wellness" (sounds niche)
- "Vedic science" (sounds pseudoscientific to skeptics)
```

## Overview

### What is a Lifestyle Architect?

A Lifestyle Architect is someone who can:
1. **Understand** time-tested frameworks for life design (from traditions refined over millennia)
2. **Map** those frameworks to modern behavioral science and product design
3. **Design** lifestyle protocols for different personas and life stages
4. **Build** ethical engagement systems oriented toward genuine well-being
5. **Implement** these as personal practices, coaching frameworks, or products

### Core Premise

Ancient Indian systems solved the same problem modern behavioral scientists wrestle with: **How do you design a life that is sustainable, meaningful, and leads to flourishing?**

The difference:
- **Modern approach**: Bottom-up (habits → routines → identity)
- **Ancient approach**: Top-down (purpose → duties → daily practices)

This skill teaches you to work **both directions simultaneously**.

# Phase 1: Ancient Lifestyle Blueprints

**Duration:** 3 weeks
**Theme:** Deep dive into Indian frameworks

## Week 1.1: The Four Goals and Four Stages

### Learning Objectives
- Explain the four puruṣārthas and their relationships
- Describe the four āśramas and their duties
- Understand gṛhastha dharma as the "load-bearing" stage

### Key Concepts

#### Puruṣārthas (Four Goals of Life)

| Goal | Sanskrit | Domain | Modern Parallel |
|------|----------|--------|-----------------|
| Righteousness | Dharma | Ethics, duty, cosmic order | Values, purpose |
| Prosperity | Artha | Wealth, security, resources | Career, finances |
| Pleasure | Kāma | Enjoyment, aesthetics, desire | Well-being, relationships |
| Liberation | Mokṣa | Freedom, self-realization | Meaning, transcendence |

**Key insight:** These are not sequential but simultaneous. A well-designed life serves all four, with dharma as the container.

#### Āśrama-Dharma (Four Life Stages)

| Stage | Sanskrit | Age/Phase | Primary Focus | Duties |
|-------|----------|-----------|---------------|--------|
| Student | Brahmacarya | ~5-25 | Learning | Study, celibacy, service to teacher |
| Householder | Gṛhastha | ~25-50 | Production | Family, work, social contribution |
| Forest-dweller | Vānaprastha | ~50-75 | Transition | Gradual withdrawal, mentoring |
| Renunciate | Sannyāsa | ~75+ | Liberation | Complete focus on mokṣa |

**Key insight:** Gṛhastha (householder) is where most people spend most of their lives. It's not inferior to renunciation—it's the stage that supports all others.

#### Gṛhastha Dharma (Householder Duties)

The householder is responsible for:
- **Pañca-mahā-yajña** (Five Great Sacrifices):
  1. Brahma-yajña: Study and teaching
  2. Deva-yajña: Worship and ritual
  3. Pitṛ-yajña: Honoring ancestors
  4. Manuṣya-yajña: Hospitality to humans
  5. Bhūta-yajña: Care for all beings

- **Ṛṇa-traya** (Three Debts):
  1. Debt to sages (repaid through study)
  2. Debt to ancestors (repaid through progeny/continuation)
  3. Debt to gods (repaid through ritual)

## Week 1.2: Yogic Ethics and Practice Structure

### Learning Objectives
- Internalize the yamas and niyamas as behavioral invariants
- Understand aṣṭāṅga yoga as a lifestyle architecture (not just poses)
- Define sādhanā as "life as continuous practice"

### Key Concepts

#### Yamas (Restraints) - Social Ethics

| Yama | Sanskrit | Meaning | Behavioral Implication |
|------|----------|---------|------------------------|
| Non-violence | Ahiṃsā | Not causing harm | Diet, speech, action review |
| Truthfulness | Satya | Alignment of thought, word, deed | Integrity practices |
| Non-stealing | Asteya | Not taking what isn't given | Resource consciousness |
| Moderation | Brahmacarya | Wise use of vital energy | Energy management |
| Non-grasping | Aparigraha | Not accumulating beyond need | Simplicity practices |

#### Niyamas (Observances) - Personal Disciplines

| Niyama | Sanskrit | Meaning | Behavioral Implication |
|--------|----------|---------|------------------------|
| Purity | Śauca | Cleanliness of body/mind/environment | Hygiene, space design |
| Contentment | Santoṣa | Satisfaction with what is | Gratitude practices |
| Discipline | Tapas | Heat, austerity, effort | Challenge practices |
| Self-study | Svādhyāya | Study of self and texts | Reflection, reading |
| Surrender | Īśvara-praṇidhāna | Dedication to something greater | Purpose alignment |

#### Aṣṭāṅga Yoga (Eight Limbs)

```
1. Yama (social ethics)
2. Niyama (personal observances)
        ↓
3. Āsana (posture/body)
4. Prāṇāyāma (breath)
        ↓
5. Pratyāhāra (sense withdrawal)
6. Dhāraṇā (concentration)
7. Dhyāna (meditation)
8. Samādhi (absorption)
```

**Key insight:** The first two limbs (ethics) come BEFORE physical practice. Modern yoga inverts this. Lifestyle architecture puts ethics first.

#### Sādhanā (Continuous Practice)

Sādhanā = The practice you do daily, regardless of mood or circumstance.

Characteristics:
- **Non-negotiable**: Not optional, not dependent on motivation
- **Consistent**: Same time, same place, same sequence
- **Sacred**: Elevated from "habit" to "offering"
- **Cumulative**: Benefits compound over years and decades

**Exercise 1.2.2:** Design a minimal sādhanā (15 minutes) incorporating at least one practice from each category: body, breath, mind.

## Week 1.3: Ayurvedic Rhythms and Indian Hook Models

### Learning Objectives
- Understand dinacharya (daily routine) as circadian alignment
- Understand ritucharya (seasonal routine) as environmental adaptation
- Recognize ancient "hooks": vrata, festivals, phala-śruti, saṃskāras

### Key Concepts

#### Dinacharya (Daily Routine)

| Time | Practice | Rationale |
|------|----------|-----------|
| Before sunrise | Wake up | Align with brahma-muhūrta (creative hour) |
| Morning | Elimination, hygiene, oil practices | Clear channels |
| Morning | Movement, breath, meditation | Activate and center |
| Mid-morning | Main meal | Agni (digestive fire) strongest |
| Afternoon | Work, engagement | Pitta time (action) |
| Evening | Light meal, wind-down | Kapha time (grounding) |
| Night | Sleep by 10pm | Align with natural cycles |

#### Ritucharya (Seasonal Routine)

| Season | Quality | Adjustments |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| Spring | Kapha (heavy, wet) | Lighter foods, more movement, cleansing |
| Summer | Pitta (hot, sharp) | Cooling foods, less intensity, rest |
| Monsoon | Vata (variable) | Grounding, warming, routine |
| Autumn | Vata (dry, light) | Nourishing, oiling, stabilizing |
| Winter | Kapha (cold, heavy) | Building, heavier foods, warmth |

#### Ancient Hook Models

**Vrata (Vows/Observances)**
- Time-bound commitment (one day, one month, one year)
- Clear rules and restrictions
- Defined completion and reward
- Social visibility and accountability

**Festival Calendar**
- Pre-designed variable reward cycles
- Seasonal anchors throughout the year
- Social synchronization
- Narrative and meaning embedded

**Phala-Śruti (Fruit Statements)**
- "Benefits section" in texts
- Narrative rewards for practice
- Connects action to cosmic outcomes
- Creates anticipation and motivation

**Saṃskāras (Life Rituals)**
- 16 major life transitions ritualized
- Community involvement at key moments
- Identity transformation through ritual
- Creates "chapters" in life story

**Exercise 1.3.2:** Identify 3 festivals or observances from any tradition that create "hooks" in your life. Analyze their structure.

# Phase 2: Modern Habit & Product Science

## Week 2.1: Habit Science Foundations

### Learning Objectives
- Master the habit loop (cue-routine-reward)
- Apply the Four Laws of Behavior Change
- Use the Fogg Behavior Model (B=MAP)
- Implement Tiny Habits methodology

### Key Concepts

#### The Habit Loop (Duhigg/Clear)

```
    ┌──────────────┐
    │     CUE      │ ← Trigger/prompt
    └──────┬───────┘
           ↓
    ┌──────────────┐
    │   CRAVING    │ ← Motivational force
    └──────┬───────┘
           ↓
    ┌──────────────┐
    │   RESPONSE   │ ← The habit itself
    └──────┬───────┘
           ↓
    ┌──────────────┐
    │    REWARD    │ ← Satisfaction
    └──────────────┘
           ↓
        (loops back to cue)
```

#### Four Laws of Behavior Change (James Clear)

| Law | To Build | To Break |
|-----|----------|----------|
| 1st Law (Cue) | Make it obvious | Make it invisible |
| 2nd Law (Craving) | Make it attractive | Make it unattractive |
| 3rd Law (Response) | Make it easy | Make it difficult |
| 4th Law (Reward) | Make it satisfying | Make it unsatisfying |

#### Fogg Behavior Model (B = MAP)

**Behavior = Motivation × Ability × Prompt**

All three must be present for behavior to occur.

```
High Motivation
        │
        │    ┌─────────────────┐
        │    │ Behavior Happens│
        │    │    (above line) │
        │    └─────────────────┘
        │         Action Line
        │    ┌─────────────────┐
        │    │ Behavior Fails  │
        │    │   (below line)  │
        │    └─────────────────┘
        │
Low Motivation ───────────────────────→
              Hard to Do    Easy to Do
                   (Ability)
```

**Key insight:** When motivation is low, make behavior easier. When behavior is hard, increase motivation. Always ensure prompt is present.

#### Tiny Habits Method

1. **Anchor**: After I [existing habit]...
2. **Behavior**: I will [tiny new behavior]...
3. **Celebration**: Then I will [celebrate immediately]

Example: "After I pour my morning coffee, I will write one sentence in my journal, then I will say 'I'm a writer!'"

### Exercises

**Exercise 2.1.1:** Take one habit you want to build. Apply all Four Laws systematically.

**Exercise 2.1.2:** Identify a failed habit attempt. Analyze it using B=MAP—which element was missing?

**Exercise 2.1.3:** Design 3 Tiny Habits using the Anchor-Behavior-Celebration format.

## Week 2.2: Product Psychology and Ethical Considerations

### Learning Objectives
- Understand the Hooked Model (Trigger → Action → Variable Reward → Investment)
- Recognize ethical vs. exploitative design
- Apply Minimum Effective Dose (MED) thinking
- Use periodization for sustainable change

### Key Concepts

#### The Hooked Model (Nir Eyal)

```
    ┌─────────────┐
    │   TRIGGER   │ External → Internal over time
    └──────┬──────┘
           ↓
    ┌─────────────┐
    │   ACTION    │ Simplest behavior in anticipation of reward
    └──────┬──────┘
           ↓
    ┌─────────────┐
    │  VARIABLE   │ Tribe (social), Hunt (resources), Self (mastery)
    │   REWARD    │
    └──────┬──────┘
           ↓
    ┌─────────────┐
    │ INVESTMENT  │ User puts something in (time, data, effort)
    └─────────────┘
           ↓
        (creates next trigger)
```

#### Variable Reward Types

| Type | Sanskrit Parallel | Modern Example |
|------|-------------------|----------------|
| Tribe | Satsang, community | Social media likes, leaderboards |
| Hunt | Artha pursuit | Slot machines, search results |
| Self | Sādhanā progress | Game levels, skill mastery |

#### Ethical Design Questions

Before building any hook, ask:
1. Would I use this myself?
2. Does it help users achieve their stated goals?
3. Does it build capability or dependency?
4. Can users easily disengage?
5. Is the reward genuine value or manipulation?

#### Minimum Effective Dose (MED)

The smallest dose that produces the desired outcome.

| Domain | MED Example |
|--------|-------------|
| Exercise | 2x/week strength training (vs. daily gym) |
| Meditation | 5 min/day (vs. hour-long sessions) |
| Learning | 15 min focused practice (vs. passive consumption) |
| Social | 1 deep conversation/week (vs. constant shallow contact) |

#### Periodization

Cycling intensity over time to prevent burnout and enable recovery.

```
Week 1-3: Build (70% intensity)
Week 4: Deload (50% intensity)
Week 5-7: Build (80% intensity)
Week 8: Deload (50% intensity)
Week 9-12: Build (90% intensity)
Week 13: Full rest
```

### Exercises

**Exercise 2.2.1:** Analyze one app you use daily through the Hooked Model. Map each element.

**Exercise 2.2.2:** Analyze one traditional practice (religious ritual, cultural custom) through the Hooked Model.

**Exercise 2.2.3:** For a habit you want to build, determine the MED. What's the smallest version that still works?

### Key Concepts

#### The Habit → Ritual → Culture Progression

```
HABIT                 RITUAL                 CULTURE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mechanical           Meaningful             Identity
Individual           Shared                 Collective
Optimized            Beautiful              Sacred
Tracked              Witnessed              Transmitted
"I do this"          "This is how I do it"  "This is who we are"
```

**Progression strategy:**
1. Start with habit (make it easy, consistent)
2. Add ritual elements (naming, setting, sequence, beauty)

#### Ritual Design Elements

| Element | Function | Example |
|---------|----------|---------|

| Sequence | Creates flow | Always same order |
| Objects | Creates tangibility | Candle, bell, journal, mala |
| Opening/Closing | Creates boundaries | Invocation, dedication |
| Witness | Creates accountability | Partner, group, teacher |

#### Modern Ritual Calendar Design

Layer multiple rhythms:
- **Daily**: Sādhanā (morning), reflection (evening)
- **Weekly**: Sabbath/rest day, community practice
- **Monthly**: New moon intention, full moon review
- **Quarterly**: Seasonal transition ritual
- **Annual**: Birthday review, new year intention, major festivals

## Week 4.2: Protocol Design Methodology

### Learning Objectives
- Design protocols for different capacity levels
- Apply MED and periodization
- Create sustainable progressions

### Key Concepts

#### Capacity Levels

| Level | Description | Available Energy | Complexity Tolerance |
|-------|-------------|------------------|---------------------|
| Low | Recovering, overwhelmed, depleted | Minimal | Very simple only |
| Medium | Stable, some buffer | Moderate | Some complexity OK |
| High | Thriving, surplus energy | Abundant | Can handle challenge |

#### Protocol Design Principles

1. **Start below capacity**: Leave 20-30% buffer
2. **One change at a time**: Stack only after stability
3. **Anchor to existing**: Use Tiny Habits methodology
4. **Build in recovery**: Periodization is mandatory
5. **Social by default**: Add accountability from start
6. **Meaning matters**: Connect to larger purpose
7. **Track minimally**: Enough to know, not enough to obsess
8. **Review regularly**: Weekly reflection, monthly adjustment

#### Protocol Template

```
PROTOCOL: [Name]
FOR: [Persona/life stage]
CAPACITY: [Low/Medium/High]
DURATION: [Time period]

DAILY NON-NEGOTIABLES:
- [Practice 1] (time, duration)
- [Practice 2] (time, duration)

DAILY IDEALS (when capacity allows):
- [Practice 3]
```

# Phase 5: Building Ethical Hook Systems

**Duration:** 2 weeks
**Theme:** Creating engaging systems that liberate rather than addict

## Week 5.1: Ethical Hook Design

### Learning Objectives
- Apply the Hooked model ethically
- Use variable rewards without manipulation
- Build investment loops that increase capability

### Key Concepts

#### The Dharmic Hook Test

Before implementing any hook, verify:

| Question | Required Answer |
|----------|-----------------|
| Does this serve the user's stated goals? | Yes |
| Would the user thank me for this in 10 years? | Yes |
| Does this build capability or dependency? | Capability |
| Can users easily disengage? | Yes |
| Is the reward genuine value? | Yes |
| Am I comfortable if everyone knew how this works? | Yes |

#### Ethical Variable Rewards

| Type | Exploitative Version | Dharmic Version |
|------|---------------------|-----------------|
| Tribe | Likes, followers (vanity) | Real community, genuine feedback |
| Hunt | Slot machine mechanics | Genuine discovery, useful information |
| Self | Artificial levels, badges | Real skill development, genuine progress |

#### Building Capability Through Investment

Exploitative investment: Lock-in, switching costs, sunk cost fallacy
Dharmic investment: Skill building, relationship deepening, self-knowledge

Examples of dharmic investment:
- Journaling that builds self-awareness
- Practice logs that reveal patterns
- Teaching others that deepens understanding
- Customization that reflects true preferences

## Week 5.2: System Architecture

### Learning Objectives
- Design complete Habit → Ritual → Culture progressions
- Create community structures that support practice
- Build feedback loops that inform without obsessing

### Key Concepts

#### The Progression Architecture

```
STAGE 1: HABIT (Weeks 1-4)
├── Focus: Consistency over quality
├── Hook: Simple trigger → action → immediate reward
├── Support: App reminders, basic tracking
├── Success metric: Did I do it? (binary)

STAGE 2: RITUAL (Weeks 5-12)
├── Focus: Quality and meaning
├── Hook: Anticipated experience → rich practice → delayed reward
├── Support: Community check-ins, deeper teaching
├── Success metric: How was it? (qualitative)

STAGE 3: CULTURE (Week 13+)
├── Focus: Identity and transmission
├── Hook: Internal motivation → embedded practice → meaning
├── Support: Teaching others, community leadership
├── Success metric: Who am I becoming? (identity)
```

#### Community Design Patterns

| Pattern | Structure | Function |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| Pair | 2 people | Deep accountability, daily check-in |
| Pod | 3-6 people | Weekly practice, support, challenge |
| Cohort | 10-30 people | Learning together, shared journey |
| Sangha | Ongoing community | Culture, transmission, belonging |

#### Feedback Loop Design

| Frequency | What to Track | How to Review |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|
| Daily | Did I practice? (binary) | Quick check, no analysis |
| Weekly | Quality + challenges | 10-min reflection, pattern noticing |
| Monthly | Domain scores, trends | 30-min review, adjustment planning |
| Quarterly | Life alignment, trajectory | Deep reflection, major adjustments |

## Design Principles Summary

### How Ancient Structures Inform Modern Design

1. **Purpose first**: Start with puruṣārthas (what is this life for?), not just goals
2. **Ethics as container**: Yamas/niyamas define what's off-limits before optimizing
3. **Rhythm is architecture**: Dinacharya/ritucharya show that when matters as much as what
4. **Community is infrastructure**: Satsang, saṃskāras make practice social by default
5. **Beauty elevates**: Ritual elements transform habits into meaningful practices
6. **Time horizon matters**: Design for decades, not days

### How to Keep Systems Dharmic

1. **Serve liberation, not dependency**: Does this build capability or lock-in?
2. **Genuine rewards only**: No manipulation, dark patterns, or artificial scarcity
3. **Easy exit always**: Users should be able to leave better than they came
4. **Transparency**: Would users approve if they knew how everything worked?
5. **Long-term test**: Would I be proud of this in 10 years?

### How to Adapt for Different Capacities

1. **Low capacity**: Reduce to 2-3 non-negotiables, remove all complexity
2. **Medium capacity**: Add practices gradually, maintain 20% buffer
3. **High capacity**: Full protocol with challenges, still include recovery
4. **Always**: Design "scale down" triggers before you need them
